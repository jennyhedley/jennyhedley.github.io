Jenny Hedley

Jenny Hedley’s digital poetry, creative nonfiction, memoir and essays have been published widely since returning to university to study creative writing as a mature-age student. On account of living with mental illness and becoming a single mother following domestic violence, her work is complex, raw and politically engaged.

Jenny's writing appears in Cordite Poetry Review, DIAGRAM, Mascara Literary Review, Verity La, SCUM Magazine and other publications. She recorded her poem 'I Can See Through Your Lululemons' for Memoria Podcast and was a participant in Red Room Poetry's MAD Poetry Workshop. Her poem ‘Frequency’ will appear in the anthology Admissions: Voices within Mental Health in October 2022.

A passion for coding digital literature and interactive (non)fiction led her to complete a course in JavaScript Development at General Assembly in 2022. Her interactive poetry exhibits a nineties internet aesthetic. She's the proud mother of two Twitter bots: her first Mother Bot @AnneBotWallace tweets randomly generated poetry and @Archive Fever Bot replies to those tweets nonsensically.

This year she will complete her Media and Communication (Honours) at RMIT under the supervision of Francesca Rendle-Short. She's exploring her relationship to her late mother's literary archive through a speculative lens, drawing on Derrida's archive fever, Quinn Eades's écriture matière and Kristeva's chora. This honours project forms an exergue to what will be a book-length creative work (working title: My Archive Fever).

She recently drafted an experimental anti-genre memoir My Body Remembers, where she uses a temporal spotlight to revisit key moments from her past, approaching sites of trauma through abecedarian, archive, essay, hermit crab, interactive (choose your own misadventure), list, poetry and prose form with a clarity and a candidness that resists victimhood. She confronts her demons with an acerbic literary arsenal, using humour, volatility and disruption as weapons.

Jenny lives on unceded Boon Wurrung land with her son. She acknowledges the traditional owners of this land, the Yalukit Willam people, and pays her respect to their ancestors past and present. This always was and always will be Aboriginal land.
